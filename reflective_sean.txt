I don't think the current system does a good job holding short-term rentals in San
Francisco accountable. It relies on hosts to enter their own policy numbers and on other people
to report violations, which means Airbnb isn't really checking if listings are legal before they go
up. Since enforcement mostly happens after complaints, illegal listings can stay active for a
while without being dealt with. This goes against what the SWE Code of Ethics says about
responsibility, since platforms should make sure their systems don't cause harm. A better way
would be for Airbnb to verify policy numbers with the city before a listing goes live, instead of
waiting for someone to complain after the fact.
2.
One important factor in whether web scraping is legal is what counts as "unauthorized
access." The CFAA makes unauthorized access to computer systems illegal, but it's still unclear
how that applies to scraping public websites. The hiQ vs. LinkedIn case is a good example of
this, since the court had to decide if scraping publicly visible data actually broke the CFAA. The
legality of web scraping will still be uncertain until there are clearer laws around this.
3.
One guideline is that scraping should only be done for a clear and reasonable purpose.
For example, for research or holding platforms accountable, not just to take advantage of data.
Another guideline is to be careful with how the data gets used, especially when it involves real
people. Using data in a harmful or misleading way is still wrong even if it is public. Scraping
should be done carefully and responsibly so it doesn't end up hurting anyone.
